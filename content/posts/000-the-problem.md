We have a property in Ontario on the Canadian Shield. If you've spent time in this part of the country you know what that means: exposed bedrock, rocky outcroppings, uneven ground, steep slopes, trees growing straight out of solid rock, and leaf-covered terrain that hides everything underneath.

It's beautiful. It's also brutal to work on.

![Rocky slope with exposed Canadian Shield bedrock and a small outbuilding](/images/terrain-1.jpg)

This is a typical section of the property. What looks like a gentle slope is actually a minefield of exposed rock ledges, root systems, and soft spots. Hauling anything heavy across this — firewood, tools, landscaping materials — means either a wheelbarrow that tips constantly, multiple trips on foot, or just accepting that some things don't get moved.

![Waterfront area with rocky terrain, picnic table, and wooded slope](/images/terrain-2.jpg)

The waterfront area is no better. The ground between the house and the water drops away through a rocky, root-covered slope with trees growing at awkward angles. Getting anything down there — or back up — is a workout.

## The actual problems

We have stumps. A lot of them. Removing them means either hiring equipment (expensive, and equipment access is limited by the terrain) or doing it by hand with a winch. We do the latter, which means hauling a heavy winch, chains, and snatch blocks across this terrain for every single stump.

We heat partly with wood. Firewood needs to get from where it's split to where it's stacked, across terrain like this, in quantities that make a wheelbarrow feel like a cruel joke.

In winter, we get real Ontario snowfall. The paths between buildings need to be cleared. The pond freezes and the kids want to skate, which means Zamboni duty — flooding the surface with water, by hand, in the cold.

And throughout all of it, there's the simple problem of carrying things. Tools, materials, groceries from the car to the house, gear to the dock. It all has to cross this terrain, carried by humans, one trip at a time.

## What we actually need

A utility carrier that can:

- Handle Canadian Shield terrain — rocks, roots, slopes, soft ground
- Carry 200–250 kg of payload without tipping or bogging
- Pull stumps via a front winch
- Move slowly and safely around kids, dogs, and other obstacles
- Eventually follow me around the property autonomously so I'm not playing fetch with a wheelbarrow all day
- Fit in the back of a Model Y so I can bring it to the property

Nothing commercial does all of this at a reasonable price. So I'm building it.

That's E-Yor.
